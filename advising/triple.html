Considerations

I offer the following editorial comments.

On "triple-counting"

No satisfying arrangement of triple-majors requires any course to count toward more than two majors.

A considerable number of policy changes would be necessary to require triple-counting. The +7 Mathematics specifically exercises two courses in the Computer Science and one course in the Data Science major that otherwise counted uniquely toward that major. Further, only a single Mathematics course other than the required courses may be counted toward the Data Science major, and the Data Science major requires no additional electives in the +7 case. Currently, all 19 courses in the triple-major may be counted toward Computer Science major requirements, allowing extreme flexibility to avoid any triple-counting conditions.

On CS__ 151_

In the common case, CS__ 151_ counts toward all three majors as an enumerated requirement. Historically it has been my personal preference to do so (and the preference of the registrar), but it may be counted toward solely Computer Science and Data Science by applying the distribution allowed in the Mathematics major.

On MATH 239_

Historical class MATH 239_ may raise some interesting questions with regards to triple-counting. With respect to requirements, it fulfills a similar position to MATH 280_, an unusually high-value class in that it is a Data Science major requirement (or was, prior to the 2023 catalog) and may count as an elective toward the Mathematics major at 200-level.

  • The most similar course in terms of requirements and department, MATH 280_ is not triple-counted, does not need to be triple-counted, and is often not counted at all in the 15+5 track.
  • The most similar course in terms of learning objectives per the "Guidelines for Advising Curriculum Transition", DATA 151_, is not double-counted nor triple-counted under 12+7, 14+5, or balanced arrangements.
  • The next most similar course in terms of requirements and department, MATH 253_, is double-counted under the balanced arrangement but single-counted under 12+7 and 14+5 arrangements.
  • Generally, without triple-counting the Computer Science major has access to 7 courses which can be counted as electives and are only counted toward a single major, so any arrangement in which MATH 239_ is triple-counted should be resolvable by removing MATH 239_ from the Computer Science major and making any replacement.

On Other Multiple Majors

∃ a 18-course triple-major with Economics that utilizes no triple-counting, primarily due to the Economics major requiring only 8 courses. If the Economics department does not apply the substitutions recommended by the "Guidelines for Advising Curriculum Transition", this is raised to a 22-course triple-major with the retirement of MATH 239_. This is known to students, but among our majors most are opting for the Mathematics program instead.

∃ a 21-course triple-major with Public Health that utilizes no triple-counting. If the Public Health department does not apply the substitutions recommended by the "Guidelines for Advising Curriculum Transition", this is raised to a 19-course triple-major with the retirement of MATH 239_. I believe we should offer an ethics distribution within our majors that accepts PHEAL 301 Public Health Ethics and drops this to a 20-course triple-major, where PHEAL 499W Senior Seminar in Public Health fulfills the upper-level writing requirement.

∃ 23-course (CS__/DATA) and 24-course (CS__/MATH) triple-majors with Physics that become impossible without triple-counting, as CS__ 151_ is a requirement for all three majors. There exist undeclared first-years considering this track, which may also become impossible due to scheduling conflicts between courses or lack of general education offerings that span multiple requirements.

∃ a 25-course quadruple major that requires no triple-counting.

∃ synthetic triple-majors that require fewer than 19 courses using a combination of the the 2022 Catalog, the "Guidelines for Advising Curriculum Transition", and novel policies around capstones and electives. These synthetic programs ambigiously utilized triple-counting depending on how course substitutions are applied. This track is primarily eliminated by the new 2023 requirement of CS__ 271_ within the Computer Science major, and will remain inactive so long as the Data and Computing majors require at least 6 specific courses per major, as they did beginning in 2023.

On Computer Science elective credit for Mathematics courses

The 12+7 track demonstrates that a 19-course triple-major without triple-counting is possible regardless of whether Mathematics courses bear Computer Science elective credit. My assessment of the primary impact of standarizing Mathematics credit is to equalize opportunity with respect to the 15-course double major for students experiencing different topics scheduling after they have completed MATH 251W.

A 15-course double major is consistent with internal CAS double majors with mathematical requirements, such as Economics/Philosophy (15) or Public Health/Environmental Science (16).

On Policy Changes

Impact

The 12+7 track is unaffected any of the three following conditions or their combinations:

  • Allowing Computer Science credit for Mathematics courses
  • Offering MATH 239_
  • Allowing triple-counting.
Should Mathematics courses no longer bear Computer Science elective credit, historical class MATH 239_ will be reduced from having a minimum of 7 to having a minimum of 1 alternatives for Computer Science major elective credit, but still not necessitate triple-counting.

Administrative Burden

I believe the strongest argument in defense of the existing arrangement is how easy it is to advising and to staff. I am aware of no other proposal that are competitive with the existing arrangement in this regard. Imposing an upper-level Computer Science elective requirement, for example, will minimally require staffing these electives once per year to preserve a 4-semester track through the major. However, Computer Science courses overwhelming experience scheduling collisions, and frequently must be cancelled due to underenrollment. We were able to staff no Computer Science elective with Data Structures dependency this year. With an upper-level requirement, this would have blocked students beginning their third year in Fall 2021 from finishing the program on time, in conflict with our access goals.

Similarly, advising is a major burden on faculty within our program, and complex arrangements enforcing conditional requirements, such as courses that may count for major requirements based on whether they are counted for other requirements outside of the major, will be severely taxing on advising time and create conditions under which expected levels of human errors will endanger students graduation preparedness. In fact, an sufficiently advanced policy should be considered unenforcable itself due to likelihood of human error.

Computer Science elective credit for Mathematics courses, in particular, has greatly reduced the burden on Computer Science advisors to find courses that Computer Science majors may take toward their major and fit toward their schedule. Restrictions on the pool of available classes directly convert to additional workloads for faculty and access restrictions on the marginally schedulable student. This is the resource we are most eager to conserve and the students we are most committed to retain.

On Rigor

Individuals

I am not unaware of concerns that a 19-course triple-major may be considered to demonstrate a lack of rigor in the major requirements. I differ from that assessment. I believe the triple-major is a path to rigor while under University conditions, which include staffing and general education considerations, that renders impractical the level of rigor our students aspire to within a single field. Rather, the 19-course triple-major provides students with a framework within which they can delve deeply into mathematical sciences, an already difficult subject area for specialization at the undergraduate level. Students emerge with strong preparation in each of Computer Science, Data Science, and Mathematics, and as competitive candidates for industry or graduate-level study in any of these fields. While it is out of scope for this discussion, I question concerns about rigor when a student takes 19-courses, if there are not accompanying concerns for the rigor of the education of a student that takes 10-course major. Even the 12-course double major, an unusually challenging 12-courses due to major requirements, offers a much higher floor for rigor.

In my assessment, students completing our program, compared to their peers from teaching institutions or research institutions, are graduating as more competitive scholars. Willamette triple-majors are credibly competitive with technically-focused R1 scholars while additionally bearing the benefits of a liberal arts education - we may simply consider the number of hours of technical instruction they receive. I believe it is unreasonable to claim one of our triple-majors has not demonstrated the capacities that would be expected of a single-major Mathematics major, a single-major Data Science major, or a single-major Computer Science major. We are developing exceptional interdisciplinary scholars, who are exactly what our fields today demand.

Populations

At population level, the popularity of multi-major plans-of-study elevates the expected quality of instruction for students from our program. Simply put, faculty have the opportunity to deliver more instructional hours in computing and data as students pursue more majors. Without more information, it is is difficult to conclude if we have arrived at the optimal arrangements for maximizing expected instructional hours, so I will make no claims to optimality but will say I have been pleased with the reception our program has received and the popularity of these plans-of-study among our students. As a point of comparison, we currently serve 37 computer science majors, up from 20 in August '22 and 27 in May '22.

Access & Equity

It is my position that the existing 19-course triple-major is more rigorous than pre-existing configurations possible under earlier versions of the catalog. In fact, less demanding triple-majors were possible as recently as the 2022 catalog. The rising popularity of the triple-major is not the result of a decrease in the rigor of our program, therefore, but necessarily some other factor. As a supporting line of argument, consider the depth of discussion that has surrounded the demanding 12+7 triple-major, while a 12+6 triple-major with Economics is virtually unutilized. Students are selecting into more rigorous programs as they develop the skills to succeed in more challenging classes by enrolling in the courses offered by our program.

I find the rising popularity of the triple-major is consistent with our access goals as a program and our current advising model, which is markedly more accessible, I believe, than any of our peer majors at Willamette or, in fact, a liberal arts colleges generally. It is my view that in 2022 and earlier catalog years, subgroups within the student population discovered and enacted plans-of-study that satisfied the requirements of three majors with less-than-or-equal rigor than students following the 2023 12+7 track. Students in these subgroups entered Willamette with significant unearned advantages and the existing advising model compounded these advantages by granting additional credentials compared to their no-less-brilliant but less-advantaged peers. These same students have seen stunning career success, and while this must be attributed to initial advantages in part, appears correlated with the quality of instruction they received through intensive, multi-major study.
