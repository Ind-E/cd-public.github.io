Assessing and Predicting the Effect of Scholarships on Student Success

It is known that first-time full-time (FTFT) freshmen students are highly vulnerable to the many sources of attrition embedded in the usual university experience. Negative responses to attrition include first-year desertion or failure to graduate within six years of beginning a four-year undergraduate program. In this talk, I will discuss applied research on FTFT students at Oregon State University (OSU), conducted alongside a team of interested parties in the College of Science at OSU. Using data from the OSU Office of Institutional Reporting and Analytics, we specifically aim to understand how increases in gift aid - that is, financial assistance that is not repaid, impacts these students' first-year retention rates. We discuss results indicating that achievement gaps between students with different demographics (e.g. Pell-eligibility) can be narrowed through the strategic awarding of gift aid. We also discuss the predictive component of the problem: modeling strategies that moved us beyond simple inferential models, and the issue of overlap and imbalance. We found that features that should intuitively be explanative of retention or desertion were very similar across all FTFT students (overlap). We also found that because of the few number of students who deserted, the amount of information we presented to our models on this class was limited (imbalance). These data difficulties presented a challenge to the binary classification task, and I discuss my approach to these challenges, known solutions to them, and why future work is needed to tackle a combination of these challenges.

Pop Star: AI and Machine Learning for Lyrical Music Generation

Research has shown that repetition in popular music (e.g., music with repeating motifs, form structure, and lyrical phrases) is associated with greater market success. While state-of-the-art AI systems are very effective at generating artifacts with strong local cohesion, generating artifacts with the long-term dependencies needed to create repetition remains an open problem. Using modified sequence alignment algorithms and constrained Markov models, we have designed a system—affectionately called Pop* (Pop Star)—capable of generating lyrical music that exhibits both locally cohesive elements as well as the motifs, forms, and rhyming that have made popular music so successful. A song composed by Pop* won 10th place overall in the 3rd International AI Song Contest in 2022 as judged by a panel of AI experts, professional recording artists, and a public vote. Come learn more about Dr. Bodily's research in the field of computational creativity and the potential for using such systems for therapeutic purposes.

Best Practices

During the '21-'22 Computer Science search, I reached out to some colleagues about hiring best practices.

  • My faculty mentor: Professor Cooper Battle
  • The faculty dean: Professor Rachel Kinsman Steck (they/she)
  • A domain expert: Professor Melissa Buis Michaux (she)

What follows are general recommendations from our communications.

From colleagues

Professor Melissa Buis Michaux, Politics, Policy, Law and Ethics

  • "Be more explicit about whether the candidates demonstrate an understanding and/or have experience with building an inclusive classroom environment."
  • "State more specific credentials or skills that you want to see."
  • "Look for evidence that candidates are able to connect with, mentor, or otherwise reach underserved students."

From colleagues

Professor Cooper Battle, Chemistry

  • "Asking people to rank specific dimensions (engagement, enthusiasm, interaction, etc.) might be a better approach."
  • "More general evaluative rankings might be opaque with regards to what the respondent is measuring."
  • "Perspectives on particular dimensions of performance that the [Hiring Committee] has identified as critical to the job might give you all more information to decide based on."

From colleagues

Professor Rachel Kinsman Steck, Theatre and Women's & Gender Studies

  • "Tie directly back to a rubric stemming from the job description and how the search committee is prioritizing the job description."
  • "If it is not in the job description, the committee should not be considering the merits."
  • "Ask for a rank for teaching demonstration and research demonstration; then ask for specific observations that support the ranking."

Position Descriptions

In addition to the Affirmative Action/Equal Employment Opportunity language that must be included in all job postings, consider including a statement that emphasizes your interest in diversifying your faculty. Some examples are:

  • “The Department is keenly interested in diversifying its faculty and encourages applications from diverse candidates, [especially candidates of color and/or women”
  • “The Department values diversity among its faculty, is committed to building a culturally diverse intellectual community, and strongly encourages applications from women and minorities[sic].”
  • Many universities ask prospective job candidates to describe “experience working with and teaching diverse students.” Doing so provides useful information and signals the committee’s interest in recruiting a diverse pool of applicants.

Committees

  • Imbalances of power among committee members can silence some members while allowing for others to control too much of the search process.
  • [Senior faculty] intimidate untenured faculty on the committee. Untenured faculty may feel uncomfortable disagreeing with their senior colleagues who later will be evaluating them.
  • Encourage an open airing of ideas and opinions by all committee members on the topic of excellence and diversity.
  • Consider forming a student advisory group to work with the faculty search committee. Experience shows that students—who are early career themselves—can be especially good at identifying emerging scholars
  • [There should be] a committee member to be responsible for ensuring that there is broad outreach designed especially to attract [applicants of color and/or women].

Bias Risk Factors 1/2

The following is adapted from text on unconscious bias. Absent any evidence that the bias is unconscious, I have shifted all passive voice to active voice.

  • Candidates of color and/or women are held to higher standards. Search committee members scrutinize their records in a dismissive way, evaluate the same achievements as others to be less important, and fail to notice unexpected achievements.
  • Search committee members minimize the contributions by candidates of color and/or women, and attribute success to mentors and collaborators.
  • Be especially vigilant about statements concerning “fit.” This euphemism is often used to exclude individuals whose demographic characteristics don’t match the demographics of the department or field.

Bias Risk Factors 2/2

The following is adapted from text on unconscious bias. Absent any evidence that the bias is unconscious, I have shifted all passive voice to active voice.

  • Another concept to scrutinize is “potential.” Since decisions about early career stage scholars often depend upon predictions about potential, superficial qualities such as dress or style of speech should not color assessments of actual achievements.
  • Achievements of people of color and/or women are evaluated less positively than white men of equal accomplishment.

Campus Visits

Best practices emphasize the importance of good campus visits.

  • Make sure that all talks are well attended. There is nothing more depressing for a candidate than a poor turnout or few questions. Enlist students to attend and ask questions (and be sure to solicit their feedback).
  • Everyone interviewing candidates should behave appropriately and communicate respect for candidates and their time... Be especially careful about what happens outside the formal interview, particularly at meals, when everyone may be less on guard.

Some Comments

On job requirements.

  • Diversity is primarily considered only up to the point of hire.
    • Of note, the candidates are asked for public talks only on teaching and research.
  • Faculty review primarily concerns matters unmentioned prior to review.
  • These standards are often set by trustees or administrators, who are not in classrooms, and not students or faculty, who are.

On process.

  • On campus interviews occur after a "first round interview".
  • As a rule, I hear that a (relevant) Ph.D. is the qualitification for faculty position.
    • Consequently, everything else is underdefined.
